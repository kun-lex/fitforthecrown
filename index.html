SECTION 1

Your Changing Body

Puberty usually starts between 8–14 for girls and 9–15 for boys. Everyone's timing is different — earlier or later than your friends is still normal.

  • Girls: breasts develop, hips widen, monthly periods begin. A typical cycle is 21–35 days. Periods are healthy, never shameful.
  • Boys: voice deepens, rapid growth, night-time emissions are a normal part of development — not illness.
  • Everyone: pimples, sweat and mood swings arrive. Bathe daily, drink water, and remember: feelings are chemical, actions are choices.
Rule of the crown: if anything feels wrong or painful — especially period pain that stops school — tell a health worker. Asking is wisdom.
SECTION 2

Food & Fitness

Adolescence is your biggest growth season after infancy. What you eat now builds your bones, brain and blood.

  • Eat the rainbow you can afford: beans, eggs, fish, ugu, ewedu, fruit. Iron matters especially for girls.
  • Water over soft drinks — sugar crashes are real, and so are dental bills.
  • Move 60 minutes daily: football, dancing, skipping all count.
  • Sleep 8–10 hours. Your brain files what you learned in class while you sleep.
Watch out: skipping meals to look a certain way, or energy drinks as food, damage a growing body. If eating feels like fear, talk to someone on the Help page.
SECTION 3

Your Mind Matters

Strong people are not people without feelings — they are people who know what to do with them.

  • Name it: “I am anxious about WAEC” shrinks the fear. Unnamed feelings grow.
  • Talk: one honest conversation beats a hundred hidden worries.
  • Rest from screens — comparison steals joy.
  • Pray, journal, walk, sing: quiet habits that reset a loud mind.
More than a bad week? If sadness lasts most of the day for over two weeks, or you have thoughts of harming yourself — this is a health matter, like malaria, and it is treatable. Use the SOS button today, not someday. You matter more than you currently feel.
SECTION 4

Staying Safe

  • Your body belongs to you. Nobody — relative, teacher, neighbour, leader — may touch you in ways that feel wrong or secret.
  • “This is our secret” about touching, photos or gifts is a warning sign. Always.
  • Abuse is never the victim's fault. Not the clothes. Not the visit. Never.
  • The Child Rights Act and VAPP Act protect you. Child marriage and abuse are offences — reporting is your right.
If something has happened: tell a trusted adult and keep telling until someone acts. After an assault, go to a hospital or Sexual Assault Referral Centre before washing — then use the SOS numbers.
SECTION 5

Pressure & Choices

  • Substances: codeine, tramadol, cannabis and alcohol harm a developing brain faster than an adult one. Real friends accept your No the first time.
  • Cults & gangs: they recruit with belonging and pay with fear. The honourable exit is never entering.
  • Online: never share intimate photos — of yourself or anyone. Once sent, you no longer control them, and sharing such images of minors is a serious crime.
  • Fast money: your talent, compounded by patience, outruns any shortcut.
Crown-fit reply for pressure moments: “I'm good — my crown dey fit already.” Then leave. You owe nobody an explanation for protecting your future.
SECTION 6

Facts, Not Rumours

  • Pregnancy can happen from a single act, at any time of the month, the very first time. “Standing up” or “washing” prevents nothing — myths.
  • STIs, including HIV, often show no symptoms at first. Testing is the only way to know — free or cheap at government health centres.
  • Waiting is a valid, powerful choice. Pressure itself is the red flag.
  • Nobody may marry you off as a child. If you or a friend faces this, it is reportable.
  • Teenage pregnancy is a top reason girls leave school in Nigeria. Protecting your education protects two futures.
Real questions deserve real answers: ask a doctor, nurse, or youth-friendly clinic — not the playground.
